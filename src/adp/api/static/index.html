Reading the physical economy
before the financials catch up.

When factories run, they draw power; when goods move, trains and ports register it. That activity is visible weeks before it lands in a quarterly result. This platform turns those public physical-economy records into a tradable signal — and proves out the idea on history, without ever peeking at the future.

Today's signal

Every stock in the universe gets one number from the chosen factor. A higher number means the physical activity behind that company is accelerating; a lower (or negative) number means it is slowing. The list is ranked — the strategy buys the top names and sells the bottom ones.

“As of” is a time machine: you only see data that was actually published on or before that date.

Did the idea actually work?

We replay history one month at a time. On each date the strategy may only use information that was public then — so the result below is what you would genuinely have earned, not hindsight.

Runs live and fetches prices, so it takes a few seconds.

The universe

The companies the strategy can trade. Each is tied to a state, which is how a state-level power or freight reading becomes a company-level signal.

Feature explorer

The raw rows behind the signal. Each row carries three dates — this triplet is the whole point of the platform and is explained below the table.

The three dates, in plain words

feature date
the day the measurement is about.
published date
the day that measurement first became public. The backtest is only allowed to use a row once this day has passed.
as-of date
the day our pipeline computed and stored it.

Keeping these apart is what stops a strategy from “knowing” a number before the world did — the single most common way backtests lie.

How to read this

Factor
A recipe that turns raw physical data into one comparable number per company (e.g. year-on-year change in industrial power use).
Point-in-time
Every read is filtered to what was public at a chosen date, so a backtest cannot accidentally use the future.
Long / short
Buy the strongest names, sell the weakest. Returns come from the gap between them, not the market's overall direction.
CAGR
The smoothed annual growth rate of the strategy. Net is after trading costs; gross is before.
Sharpe
Return earned per unit of bumpiness. Higher is steadier. Roughly: below 1 is weak, around 1 is decent, above 2 is strong.
Information Coefficient (IC)
How well the signal's ranking lined up with what actually happened next, period by period. Small but consistently positive is good; the t-stat says whether it is more than luck.
